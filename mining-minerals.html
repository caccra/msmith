Mining Licence Types Under Uganda's Mining Act

Uganda's mining sector is primarily regulated by the Mining Act 2003 and the Mining Regulations 2004, administered by the Directorate of Geological Survey and Mines (DGSM) within the Ministry of Energy and Mineral Development. The Act establishes a tiered licensing system with four main categories of mineral rights: a Reconnaissance Licence (allowing non-intrusive surveys over large areas); a Prospecting Licence (allowing exploration work including drilling, subject to an approved work programme); a Location Licence (an intermediate right allowing more detailed investigation of a specific area); and a Mining Lease (the full production right, granting exclusive rights to mine and sell minerals from a defined area for an initial term of up to 21 years, renewable).

Artisanal and small-scale mining (ASM) operations require a separate Artisanal Mining Permit, which covers a smaller defined area and is typically granted to Ugandan citizens or entities. The formalisation of ASM has been a priority for the government in recent years, and we assist artisanal operators with obtaining the correct permits, structuring their operations for regulatory compliance, and resolving the land access issues that frequently arise in artisanal mining areas. For large-scale operations, we advise on the negotiation of Mining Development Agreements with the Government of Uganda, which can provide greater stability of terms and protections against legislative changes during the life of the project.

Uganda's mineral wealth is substantial and diverse, encompassing gold (particularly in Karamoja and western Uganda), cobalt, iron ore, copper, vermiculite, phosphates, and rare earth elements. The country's relatively underdeveloped mining sector presents significant opportunities for early investors willing to navigate the regulatory framework carefully. We advise on the full mining licence application process — from preparing the initial application and work programme through to securing the licence, meeting reporting obligations, and managing renewals and transfers.

Environmental Compliance and Community Engagement in Mining

All mining operations in Uganda must comply with the National Environment Act 2019 and the regulations of the National Environment Management Authority (NEMA). Before commencing any significant mining activity, an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) must be conducted and approved by NEMA. The EIA process involves scoping, data collection, impact prediction, mitigation planning, and public consultation — including engagement with local communities that may be affected by the proposed operations. An Environmental and Social Management Plan (ESMP) is typically required as a condition of EIA approval, setting out how identified impacts will be managed throughout the operational period. Failure to obtain or comply with the conditions of an EIA certificate can result in closure of operations and significant penalties.

Community Development Agreements (CDAs) are increasingly expected by regulators, financiers, and communities themselves as a condition of commencing large-scale mining operations in Uganda. A well-drafted CDA establishes a framework for the company's obligations to the local community — including employment and procurement preferences, contributions to community development funds, resettlement and compensation arrangements for affected households, and grievance mechanisms. We draft and negotiate CDAs on behalf of mining companies, ensuring that commitments made are commercially reasonable, legally enforceable, and consistent with international best practice standards such as the IFC Performance Standards and the Equator Principles, which are often required by project lenders.

Royalty obligations under Uganda's Mining Act require licensees to pay royalties on the gross value of minerals extracted at rates set by the Minister. Gold, for example, attracts a 3% royalty, while cobalt and other strategic minerals may attract different rates. Royalty payments are made to the Uganda Revenue Authority, and records of production must be kept and submitted regularly. In addition to royalties, mining companies are subject to Uganda's corporate income tax regime, withholding taxes on payments to contractors and expatriate employees, and VAT on local purchases — all of which require careful tax planning to manage the total fiscal burden on the project effectively.

Frequently Asked Questions

  • What licences are required to mine in Uganda?

    Depending on the scale and stage of operations, you may need a prospecting licence, location licence, or mining lease, all issued by the Directorate of Geological Survey and Mines. For artisanal mining, a separate artisanal mining permit is required. We guide clients through the correct licence type and application process for their specific situation.

  • How are mineral royalties calculated in Uganda?

    Mineral royalties under Uganda's Mining Act are calculated as a percentage of the gross value of minerals produced, with rates varying by mineral type. Gold, for example, attracts a 3% royalty. We advise on royalty obligations, reporting requirements, and tax planning strategies compliant with Uganda's mining fiscal regime.

  • What are the environmental obligations for mining operations in Uganda?

    Mining operators must conduct an Environmental Impact Assessment (EIA) approved by the National Environment Management Authority (NEMA), prepare an environmental management plan, and comply with environmental monitoring requirements throughout the operation. We advise on environmental compliance and help clients establish the necessary frameworks before commencing operations.
